The BOM's prediction of Melbourne's weather mainly relies on the combination of the observation network and the numerical weather forecast model. BOM will use ground/automatic weather stations to observe temperature, humidity, wind, air pressure and rainfall, and combine weather radar, satellite cloud images and high-altitude sounding data. Then, this information will be input into numerical forecasting systems such as ACCESS to simulate future atmospheric evolution and support operational forecasting.
The rainfall in Melbourne is influenced by both local weather conditions and borad climate scale factors.
synoptic scale: cold fronts, troughs and low-pressure systems moving across southeastern Australia
borader climate scale: ENSO, the Indian Ocean Dipole (IOD), the Southern Annular Mode (SAM)
rainy days occur on roughly 40% of days on average, equivalent to about 150 rainy days per year over the 2011–2021 period
winter has the highest rain frequency, followed by spring and autumn, while summer is the driest season.
There is a significant interannual fluctuation in the number of rainy days and annual rainfall in Melbourne, and there is no particularly simple upward and downward trend from year to year.
cool-season rainfall has generally declined over recent decades, especially in southern Australia, partly linked to changes in large-scale circulation such as the strengthening of the subtropical ridge, while natural variability from ENSO, IOD and SAM continues to play an important role.
